The Art of Video Editing: Crafting Stories Through Motion

Abdul Rahman K. Conteh March 13, 2025 6 min read

Video editing is often called the 'invisible art'—when done right, the audience doesn't notice the cuts, the pacing, or the color grading; they simply feel the story. With over eight years of experience editing for broadcast, corporate clients, and social media, I've learned that technical skills are only half the equation. The other half is understanding how to weave emotion, rhythm, and narrative into every frame.

1. The Editor as a Storyteller

Before touching any software, I immerse myself in the footage and the director's vision. The best edits arise from a deep understanding of the story's core. I ask: Who is the protagonist? What is the conflict? Where does the emotional peak occur? Every cut should serve the narrative, not distract from it. In documentary work, this means finding the moments that reveal character; in commercials, it's about building desire or urgency in 30 seconds.

2. My Editing Workflow

Over the years, I've refined a workflow that balances efficiency with creative exploration. Here's a glimpse into my process:

  • Organization: I label every clip, create bins for scenes, and use markers. A tidy timeline saves hours.
  • Assembly Cut: I lay out all the selects in order, ignoring fine-tuning. This gives me a 'radio cut' to assess story flow.
  • Fine Cut: Now I trim, adjust pacing, and add J‑cuts/L‑cuts to make dialogue feel natural.
  • Audio & Sound Design: I balance levels, add ambience, and use sound effects to build depth. Audio carries half the emotional weight.
  • Color Grading: I create a consistent look—whether it's a gritty documentary or a vibrant commercial. DaVinci Resolve is my go‑to for grading.

A complex timeline in Adobe Premiere Pro with multiple video and audio tracks.

3. The Role of AI in Modern Editing

AI tools have become indispensable in my workflow—not to replace creativity, but to remove drudgery. For example, I use Adobe Sensei for auto-reframe when adapting horizontal footage for vertical shorts. Runway ML helps with rotoscoping in minutes instead of hours. And Topaz Video AI can upscale archival footage to HD, breathing new life into old material. The key is to let AI handle repetitive tasks so I can focus on storytelling.

“Editing is not a technical process; it's an emotional one. You have to feel the rhythm.” — Thelma Schoonmaker

4. Essential Tips for Aspiring Editors

  1. Learn the rules, then break them. Understand continuity editing, but don't be afraid to use a jump cut if it serves the story.
  2. Watch films with an analytical eye. Pause and ask: Why did the editor cut here? How does the music affect the scene?
  3. Seek feedback early. Show rough cuts to trusted peers. Fresh eyes catch what you've become blind to.
  4. Build a library of resources. Stock footage, sound effects, and music tracks can save days of work.

5. The Future: Real‑Time Collaboration & VR

Cloud‑based editing platforms like Frame.io are transforming how teams work together. I can now share a cut with a client in Tokyo, and they leave comments directly on the timeline. Meanwhile, VR and 360° video demand new editing paradigms—spatial audio, 360° transitions, and interactive storytelling. As editors, we must keep learning, but the core skill—empathy for the audience—remains timeless.
